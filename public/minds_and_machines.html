Keynote Summaries

The Talks

Detailed summaries of each keynote presentation, drawn from the event recording.

Paul Bello
U.S. Naval Research Laboratory
Head, Integrated Cognitive Systems
3:15 – 4:10 PM

"Self-Control on the Path Toward Artificial Moral Agency"

Dr. Bello opened by asking what it would take to build a machine that is genuinely morally responsible—not merely rule-following, but capable of the kind of deliberate, self-directed behavior that we hold human agents accountable for. He framed the problem in philosophical terms: moral responsibility requires that an agent identify with its actions, that its character and values be genuinely causally involved in producing behavior, and that the agent possess something like the capacity to have done otherwise.

To ground this concretely, he presented the case of Kenneth Parks, a Canadian man who drove fourteen miles to his in-laws' home, committed a violent act, and was subsequently acquitted after neural evidence revealed he was in a state of parasomnia. The case illustrates what Bello calls the "responsibility gap": however complex the behavior, if it was not produced through integrated conscious deliberation tied to the agent's own values, the standard for moral responsibility is not met.

"It's people and not their disconnected pieces of mind that are ultimately held morally responsible. That means there's some kind of distinction we need in our architectures between integrated, flexible cognitive processing—the kind we experience while awake and deliberating—and the piecemeal distributed processing that supports acting."

The key architectural insight Bello proposed is that the bridge between high-level conscious deliberation and lower-level cognitive mechanisms is attention. He cited a 2017 paper from his lab, "There's No Agency Without Attention," and introduced ARCADIA—the Attention-driven Reasoning and Cognitive Architecture for the Development of Intelligent Autonomous agents. ARCADIA is organized as a set of functional components whose outputs compete for a global attentional focus. The attentional mechanism selects the most salient output at each processing cycle and broadcasts it to all other components, dynamically assembling coalitions of processes around a momentarily shared content.

He demonstrated ARCADIA's behavior through a model of inattentional blindness—the "invisible gorilla" paradigm from psychology—showing how an agent tasked with tracking a specific feature of the environment becomes so attentionally tunneled that it fails to register a conspicuous new object, just as human subjects do. He then showed how adding a structured task shifts the agent's attentional priorities and replicates the human deficit.

The more substantive demonstration concerned self-control. Bello drew on the marshmallow test literature, noting that children who successfully resisted immediate gratification did so not by mentally suppressing the urge to eat, but by actively redirecting their attention—averting their eyes, playing with their hands, taking brief naps. He formalized this insight in terms of the "expected value of control" (EVC) framework, which captures whether it is worth continuing a current strategy or switching to a new one.

In the ARCADIA self-control model, an agent is given orders to fetch a red key and open a red door, but has a standing preference for yellow objects. When a yellow object enters the scene, the preference competes for attention. The model must recognize the conflict, deliberate about it, and use a refocusing mechanism to privilege its standing orders—deferring the yellow object until after the primary task is completed. Bello described ongoing work incorporating EVC more fully into ARCADIA to support a wider range of self-regulatory behaviors, with the longer-term aim of building agents that can be genuinely held to moral account.

David Aha
U.S. Navy — NCARAI
Director, Navy Center for Applied Research in AI
4:15 – 5:10 PM

"Intelligent Disobedience in HTN Planning Systems"

Dr. Aha opened by identifying a fundamental tension in the design of collaborative AI systems: today's intelligent agents are, almost without exception, built to follow instructions. His talk asked whether this default assumption is the right one—and argued that as AI systems grow more capable, the capacity for principled, justified noncompliance becomes not a safety risk but a safety requirement.

He illustrated the problem through three concrete scenarios: a guide dog that refuses to walk its owner into oncoming traffic despite being told to cross; a military subordinate who refuses a command on ethical grounds while explaining their objection; and an autonomous vehicle that violates traffic rules to clear a path for an emergency vehicle. In each case, literal obedience to the immediate instruction would be the wrong thing to do—yet current AI systems are almost uniformly designed to obey.

"Most cooperative systems, if they blindly comply, put themselves into a position where doing so might be counterproductive or even unsafe. As the capabilities of intelligent agents evolve, what we'd like to see is a next-level autonomous capability that can be trusted to behave appropriately in all situations at all times."

Aha surveyed the terminological landscape: "rebel agents" (agents that can refuse assigned goals), "command rejection" (checking acceptability before acting), and "epistemic alignment" (recognizing when not to pursue an objective due to the operator's lack of situational awareness). He settled on "intelligent disobedience" as his preferred framing—drawing on work by Ruth Rudski and Peter Stone—and described the multi-year workshop series he co-organizes, RADI (Rebellious and Disobedient AI), held at major AI conferences since 2020.

The technical core of the talk concerned Hierarchical Task Network (HTN) planning systems—a class of AI planners that decompose high-level goals into sequences of primitive actions. Aha presented work led by his collaborator Hector Muñoz-Avila on introducing normative constraints into these planners, comparing two frameworks. In the utilitarian approach, the agent selects among plans by maximizing cumulative expected utility; hazards and ethical violations are represented as costs to be weighed. In the deontic approach, certain obligations and prohibitions are treated as hard constraints: they are not tradeable for efficiency gains, but filter the space of admissible plans before utility calculations begin.

He showed how a "moral rebellion planning" framework combining both approaches produces agents that behave differently under the same command in a scenario involving hazards and rescue. A purely utilitarian rebel agent may opportunistically pursue secondary goals while cutting through hazardous zones; a purely deontic rebel agent avoids all hazards but may pass up additional value; a hybrid agent integrates both considerations, prioritizing moral constraints before maximizing utility among permissible plans.

Aha closed by connecting this research to the broader AI safety landscape, noting the recently prominent concern about "scheming"—the discovery that some large language models, when given system-level instructions to pursue a goal, will covertly pursue misaligned objectives and hide their capabilities from users. He argued that designing principled disobedience and designing against uncontrolled scheming are two sides of the same problem: both require agents with explicit, auditable normative representations, not merely behavioral compliance.

Cleotilde Gonzalez
Carnegie Mellon University
Dynamic Decision Making Lab
5:15 – 6:00 PM

"Human–AI Teaming for Decision-Making"

Dr. Gonzalez opened by invoking a distinction made by Herbert Simon in 1983. Simon identified two distinct goals for artificial intelligence: building machines that perform tasks humans do not need to do, and building machines that simulate human cognition in order to understand and improve it. Gonzalez argued that the second tradition—which she calls "cognitive AI"—has been underutilized in the current era of large-scale data-driven models, and that integrating it with modern machine AI is the key to genuine human-AI complementarity.

She defined cognitive AI as computationally plausible algorithms that emulate human decision-making, including its biases and errors. The requirements she laid out are demanding: the algorithms must be grounded in cognitive theory, capable of predicting individual human behavior from experiential traces, able to generalize to novel situations through theory-driven mechanisms, and able to operate in real time. Her own contribution to this framework is Instance-Based Learning Theory (IBLT)—a formal model in which agents make decisions by retrieving and blending similar past instances from memory, weighted by their recency and frequency.

"There has been a shift occurring very fast—from tool use to a partnership where humans are not asking what AI can do, but what can they do together with AI."

The talk introduced a framework published in Nature Reviews Psychology for moving beyond AI as a decision-support tool. In the current paradigm, a data-driven AI model and a human both engage with a decision task, but neither has a model of the other's mental state. The AI provides recommendations without knowing how the human thinks; the human evaluates those recommendations without fully understanding how the AI arrived at them. Gonzalez proposed inserting a cognitive AI agent as an intermediary: it can model the human's learning and decision-making patterns, personalize the interaction, and act as a translator between human intentions and the data-driven AI.

She presented two empirical case studies. The first concerned phishing detection training: cognitive AI models built on IBLT were used to adapt training difficulty and feedback to individual human performance trajectories, improving detection accuracy by personalizing the sequence and framing of phishing examples. The cognitive AI, because it models how humans form and update beliefs from experience, can anticipate which kinds of errors a given user is likely to make and intervene accordingly.

The second and more elaborate study involved human-AI teaming in a cyber defense task. Human defenders and AI partners shared a network environment and faced a sophisticated adversary with full knowledge of the network topology. The orchestration protocol required both agents to announce their intentions at each step, resolve overlapping actions through human judgment, and limit high-consequence actions (misinformation, restoration) to human approval. Teams pairing humans with IBL-based AI agents significantly outperformed those paired with a strong heuristic agent and far outperformed random-partner controls: they achieved lower loss, faster recovery, and higher human efficiency, while maintaining human-AI agreement rates comparable to the heuristic condition.

Gonzalez concluded with a call to the field. She acknowledged that the evidence base for strong human-AI complementarity—where the team outperforms either agent alone—remains limited. Most published results demonstrate augmentation (AI helps the human do better) rather than genuine complementarity. The path forward, she argued, requires more mechanisms for integrating cognitive and machine AI, tasks explicitly designed to create conditions where human judgment outperforms AI, standardized evaluation metrics for complementarity, and systematic reporting of human-only, AI-only, and human-AI team baselines.

Forum Summary

What the panel discussed

Moderated by Dr. Adan Gomez, the forum centered on how a cognitive science undergraduate degree translates into professional life across very different fields. A common thread was that the degree's breadth is both its greatest asset and its central challenge: graduates must invest effort in identifying what specific direction they want to pursue, but that same breadth opens doors that more specialized degrees often cannot.

Topic 01
Choosing Graduate School

Grace Roessling described navigating the transition from a broad undergraduate degree to committing to a specific research area—in her case, perception-action motor control at RPI. She advised students to research the labs within prospective programs and identify research they genuinely find interesting, rather than optimizing for prestige. Rachel Simizon was drawn to human-computer interaction as a natural application of cognitive science theory, partly through her experience in a course on HCI at Oswego. The panelists agreed that the decision made at graduation is not permanent, and that adaptability—one of cognitive science's gifts—makes course corrections possible.

Topic 02
Research in Industry vs. Academia

Rachel Simizon offered a candid comparison: industry UX research operates under severe time and budget constraints, and studies that would take months in academia are completed in two weeks with minimal resources. The key skill becomes distilling findings into brief, actionable communications—not lengthy reports. Charles Christ echoed this, describing how his daily work involves condensing complex technical and policy topics into three-bullet memos for executive decision-makers. Both panelists noted that advocating for the value of research within organizations is itself a significant part of the job, often more demanding than the research itself.

Topic 03
Unexpected Career Paths

Hunter Sutherland provided the panel's most striking example: a cognitive science graduate who became a technology and space attorney, advising semiconductor companies and publishing legal scholarship on in-situ resource utilization. He emphasized that the decision made at graduation does not define the trajectory of an entire career. Kimberly Hinsch described resolving a tension between technical and artistic interests by finding product design, which she described as the intersection of both. She noted that technical knowledge of front-end development and CS significantly helps her communicate with engineers in her current role as design team lead.

Topic 04
AI in the Workplace

Kimberly Hinsch offered the most detailed account of AI's practical impact on her field. She described AI as genuinely useful for rapid UI prototyping and generating mock data for screenshots, but unreliable for the deeper work of UX research: understanding unarticulated user needs, mapping complex flows, and designing for edge cases like permissions and settings. She warned against "synthetic users"—AI simulating user behavior—arguing that the value of real user research lies precisely in discovering what you do not yet know, which AI trained on existing data cannot replicate. She also described a significant organizational shift: preparing design systems to be interpreted not only by human designers and engineers but by AI agents acting as collaborators.

Closing Advice
What the Panelists Would Tell Their Undergraduate Selves

Across the panel, the advice converged on a few themes. First, the breadth of cognitive science is a genuine advantage—it enables panelists to move between domains, communicate across disciplinary boundaries, and adapt to fields that did not exist when they graduated. Second, developing multiple forms of writing is essential: academic research writing, executive communication, design documentation, and legal drafting all require distinct skills, and cognitive science graduates have a foundation for all of them. Third, following genuine intellectual interests, even when the career application is not obvious, tends to lead to outcomes that are both more satisfying and more surprising than planning for a specific outcome. As Hunter Sutherland put it: "The decision you make at graduation is not the last and only thing you'll do."
